i spent my last three days in philly with someone i met three days prior. each evening, we'd meet somewhere amber-tinged and trade the separate sleep-deprived days we spent in the real world for something that felt simultaneously safer and more dangerous. my observation is that we – sensitive pisces – are too attuned to each other: to what substance the other needs and having it readily available, to the shape of each other's ears, to shifts in the other's engagement.

i know better, i think, than to think this will last, though. because here's what i know about myself:

  • for me, meeting a new person is a bit like alchemy. they are this vessel i pipette the things i like about myself into, and omit the things i don't. i fold the dreams for my life into my imaginations of how this person can fit into it. the result is a limerent fever dream that lasts anywhere between 48 hours to 13 months.
  • when i'm getting to know a new person, i will fill the space with questions so there's less room for me to have to answer any; i will ask two or three follow-up questions about their siblings' four kids so i don't have to answer whether i'm close with mine. i forget that getting to know someone means letting them know you, too. (one time, forty minutes into meeting a new person, they joked, "i didn't realize this would be an interview." i didn't like being clocked like that, so that was the last time i saw them — and softened my approach from that point on.)
  • when i'm comfortable with a new person, the line graph of my honesty & insecurity will trend exponentially upward. a nervous watchfulness hums through me when i feel too known; when i've shown someone how to properly slide the metaphorical knives off the magnetic wall rack, and they know how to locate my literal spleen.
  • when i am no longer interested in this person — when they've committed cardinal sins like embarrassing me by being too brazenly themselves, or boring me to death by talking about their air fryer — i will delay telling them that for as long as possible. it could be so simple: "thank you, but no thank you." instead, i will behave in the exact opposite manner: "of course," i'll say with pained politeness, "i would love to see you again and hear the day-to-day intricacies of what you're air frying today."
  • when i've mentally committed to leaving this person, i will schedule a time block in my google calendar to do the deed. i'll work backwards to appropriately peter out our communication till that "after reflecting, i realize i'm not ready for a relationship" bullet doesn't even have a chance to spall; it'll just go straight through. or, i tell myself, they'll be able to see it coming and side step a quarter inch to save themselves the trouble of having to break out the first aid kit. this, i know, is not helping anyone — but i mask my cowardice with the stunted excuse that i haven't exercised the muscle of saying, "no."
  • when i'm inevitably disappointed or lonely or a sick combo of the two, i will repeat this cycle tenfold. i will stress myself out letting messages pile up that i can't bring myself to answer. i will find some disgusting comfort in knowing that there are people out there who are hoping to hear from me and i am not giving them what they want. i will find a false sense of safety in being the one that hurts instead of being hurt.
  • the worst, though, is when i meet someone i actually like. someone who makes me hyena-yelp laugh. someone whose mouth i can't stop looking at. someone whose attention makes me feel less like a feral, hunted creature and more like someone with an understandable past. i will, for some inexplicable reason, take every chance i can to see how far i can push them away. i will steer them towards anything or anyone else, show them how little i care or feel or wince. i will see if they come back – suspicious if they do; justified if they don't.

    • in other words, my anxious avoidance makes itself fiercely known. it nudges me to seek: "who else? what else? where else?" it palms the wound that begs me for certainty in uncertain situations, like when i dare put my truest, strangest self in someone else's hands.

    angie says california will be good for me. three weeks of perfect weather will give me space to have a think and figure out what i really want.

    but i think there's a part of me that likes not knowing what i want, and the chaos that comes along with figuring it out. i have never felt insane – but i know i am capable of doing insane things and have a history of doing them. and it's no secret: i'm reminded of the time lillian, who's witnessed my antics and still loved me for over a decade, said, "i would risk my life for you, but i would hate dating you."

    in my heart, i want to change this narrative. i tell people i chose to leave new york for philly because it's more walkable, more affordable, more my speed. the truer, devastatingly dumber truth, is i was running from a broken heart — meaning, the shame & embarrassment of not being chosen. i had spent the better part of a year chasing the potential of being loved by someone who i thought really saw me, but whose morsels of attention & affection i saved and rationed out to myself in small spoonfuls — unsure when i'd receive more. getting a taste of my own medicine was brutal and exhausting.

    since moving to philly, i've been telling myself: we're not doing that anymore; instead: pause, then choose peace. i've designed a tattoo to commit to this version of myself who believes and practices this (the only way, clearly). i have an appointment with an artist experienced in finger tattoos when i'm back in town.

    i know, though, when it really comes down to it, it's hard for me to sit with peace. it's not something i grew up knowing; i learned to live, and perhaps love, the places and people i considered "home" lightly. the pain of being uprooted numbs considerably the third, fourth, fifth time around. so when a steady, secure option offers its hand, i distrust it. i snarl like a small, scared dog, hackles raised – then sprint the other way without looking for oncoming traffic, thinking i'm saving myself.

    so maybe my three weeks here in the bay – where the weather this time of year rarely rises above 75* or dips below 60* – is where i can learn to make peace with peace. or at least where i can let myself look at it without turning away. where i can acknowledge how i get in my own way of it. where, if i'm brave, i can move towards it. and, if i'm courageous, i can walk up these oakland hills alongside it and see if it will let me bring it home.

    it's been a little over twenty-four hours since we've been apart, and i'm cautious about whether i miss them because i miss them or because i'm feeling insecure about how ambiguous we left things. they send me voice messages that i keep because their voice and laugh make the corners of my mouth turn stupidly upward. one message is so simple and honest: "why'd you have to go so soon? i want to see you!"

    that small dog in me barks: they have plenty of people to fill their time & their head. they'll forget about you by the time you're back. sic him.

    every moment is a meditation: pause. then, choose: peace. so i ruffle the dog's head and give it praise, thank you for looking out for us.

    i loosen its collar and pick up my phone. my finger hovers over the button to record a reply. i clear my voice to dislodge the growl and let sincerity speak.
